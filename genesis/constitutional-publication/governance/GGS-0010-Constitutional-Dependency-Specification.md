# GGS-0010

Title: Constitutional Dependency Specification
Status: Active
Authority: Foundation Authority
Program: GCP-0001

## Purpose

This specification defines constitutional dependency doctrine for constitutional artifacts within the Genesis Enterprise Semantic Computing Platform.

Its purpose is to determine what kinds of dependencies may exist, in which direction they may legitimately flow, which dependencies are prohibited, and how dependency legitimacy must be evaluated.

This specification defines constitutional meaning only. It does not define implementation formats, schemas, databases, APIs, validators, or automation.

## Constitutional Position

Dependency is a governed relationship of constitutional reliance.

A dependency exists when one constitutional artifact requires another artifact's standing, meaning, authority, publication, or interpretive presence in order to remain constitutionally coherent.

Dependency doctrine exists to prevent hidden reliance, authority inversion, circular governance, and publication incoherence.

## Dependency Direction

Dependencies may only flow toward higher constitutional authority.

This means lower-authority artifacts may depend on higher-authority artifacts for meaning, legitimacy, governance, or implementation guidance.

Higher-authority artifacts shall not depend constitutionally on lower-authority artifacts for their own legitimacy or defining meaning.

## Dependency Types

### Parent Dependency

A parent dependency exists when an artifact depends on the continued intelligibility of its governing or structural parent.

Direction:
Child depends on parent.

Purpose:
Preserve structural placement and contextual coherence.

### Child Dependency

A child dependency exists when a composite, milestone, or structured artifact depends on the presence of constitutionally required subordinate artifacts for completeness.

Direction:
Composite or governing artifact depends on required child completeness only where such completeness is materially constitutive of the artifact's standing.

Purpose:
Preserve whole-subject completeness.

### Reference Dependency

A reference dependency exists when one artifact materially relies on another artifact as cited constitutional context without parentage or semantic derivation.

Direction:
Referencing artifact depends on referenced artifact.

Purpose:
Preserve contextual intelligibility.

### Semantic Dependency

A semantic dependency exists when one artifact's meaning or interpretation cannot remain constitutionally coherent without another artifact's governed meaning.

Direction:
Dependent artifact relies on higher or same-level legitimate semantic source where allowed by authority doctrine.

Purpose:
Preserve semantic truth and prevent interpretive isolation.

### Implementation Dependency

An implementation dependency exists when a lower-order engineering or implementation artifact relies on higher-order specifications, standards, architecture, or governance meaning for constitutional legitimacy.

Direction:
Lower implementation authority depends upward.

Purpose:
Preserve authority fidelity.

### Publication Dependency

A publication dependency exists when publication legitimacy of one artifact requires the approved or published standing of another artifact.

Direction:
Dependent publication artifact relies on prerequisite publication artifact.

Purpose:
Preserve repository truth and publication coherence.

### Governance Dependency

A governance dependency exists when an artifact's legitimacy, lifecycle, review standing, or publication standing relies on governance rules defined elsewhere.

Direction:
Governed artifact depends on governing artifact.

Purpose:
Preserve constitutional control and legitimacy.

## Dependency Legitimacy

A dependency is legitimate only when:
- the dependency type is intelligible
- the dependency direction is constitutionally valid
- the dependency does not violate authority hierarchy
- the dependency preserves structural, semantic, governance, or publication coherence
- the dependency is explicit where material

A dependency is not legitimate merely because an author chose to mention another artifact.

## Forbidden Dependencies

The following dependencies are constitutionally forbidden:
- higher constitutional authority depending on lower authority for defining legitimacy
- dependencies that invert authority hierarchy
- dependencies that conceal true governing sources
- dependencies that create publication reliance on absent or illegitimate artifacts
- dependencies that collapse distinct relationship types into false dependency claims

## Circular Dependency Prohibition

Circular constitutional dependencies are prohibited where they prevent intelligible constitutional standing, governance clarity, or publication legitimacy.

Circular dependencies are constitutionally defective because they obscure precedence, authority, and interpretive responsibility.

No artifact set shall require mutual constitutional legitimacy in a way that prevents clear determination of governing order.

## Dependency Validation Rules

Dependency validation shall determine, at minimum:
- whether the dependency is materially real
- whether the dependency type is correctly classified
- whether dependency direction is legitimate
- whether the dependency violates authority hierarchy
- whether the dependency creates circularity
- whether the dependency relies on unpublished or illegitimate prerequisites where such standing is required

## Dependency Invariants

The constitutional dependency model shall preserve the following invariants:
- dependencies must be intelligible
- dependencies must be directionally legitimate
- dependencies may not undermine authority hierarchy
- dependencies may not create circular constitutional ambiguity
- dependencies must support audit and publication truth
- dependencies must remain platform independent and implementation independent
