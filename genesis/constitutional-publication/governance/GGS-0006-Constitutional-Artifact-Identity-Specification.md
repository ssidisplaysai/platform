# GGS-0006

Title: Constitutional Artifact Identity Specification
Status: Active
Authority: Foundation Authority
Program: GCP-0001

## Purpose

This specification defines the constitutional identity doctrine for every constitutional artifact within the Genesis Enterprise Semantic Computing Platform.

Its purpose is to define what makes a constitutional artifact the same governed subject across review, publication, lineage, succession, supersession, and retirement.

This specification defines constitutional meaning only. It does not define implementation mechanisms, serialization formats, schemas, storage systems, APIs, or automation.

## Constitutional Position

Constitutional artifact identity is the permanent governing basis by which an artifact is recognized as a distinct constitutional subject.

Identity is not a filename, storage key, markup fragment, repository path, serialization payload, or implementation handle.

Identity is the constitutional continuity of a governed subject across its lifecycle.

No future registry, publication engine, validator, compiler, AI agent, or automation system may redefine constitutional artifact identity outside the doctrine established here.

## Scope

This specification governs:
- identity doctrine
- artifact identity meaning
- identity immutability
- identity persistence

## Identity Doctrine

Every constitutional artifact shall be treated as a governed subject with distinct constitutional identity.

Identity doctrine exists to ensure that:
- constitutional subjects remain distinguishable
- publication truth remains stable
- lineage remains intelligible
- supersession remains explicit
- future automation remains constitutionally bound

Identity doctrine shall govern meaning before representation.

Representation may change. Constitutional identity shall not become ambiguous because representation changes.

## Artifact Identity

Artifact identity means the constitutional distinctness of an artifact as a governed subject of architecture, theory, standardization, governance, specification, review, publication, or assurance.

Artifact identity shall answer, at minimum:
- what constitutional subject this artifact is
- how it is distinguished from all other constitutional subjects
- how it remains intelligible across lifecycle evolution
- how it relates to predecessors, successors, and companion artifacts

Artifact identity shall be sufficient to preserve constitutional continuity independent of repository mechanics.

## Identity Immutability

Constitutional identity shall be immutable by governance rule.

Once a constitutional artifact has been recognized as a distinct governed subject, its identity shall not be redefined casually, retroactively, or implicitly.

Identity may only cease active standing through governed retirement, supersession, or successor transition. Even then, the historical identity shall remain intelligible and preserved.

Immutability of identity exists to prevent:
- constitutional ambiguity
- publication drift
- lineage fracture
- silent replacement of governed meaning

## Identity Persistence

Identity persistence means that a constitutional artifact remains constitutionally the same governed subject across legitimate lifecycle transitions unless formal supersession or successor doctrine states otherwise.

Persistence shall preserve:
- review continuity
- publication continuity
- audit continuity
- repository intelligibility
- governance intelligibility

Persistence shall not be used to disguise substantive constitutional discontinuity.

## Artifact Naming

Artifact naming means the governed human-recognizable expression by which an artifact is referred to within Genesis constitutional discourse.

Naming exists to make constitutional subjects intelligible to humans.

Naming shall support:
- distinct recognition
- milestone comprehension
- repository discovery
- review clarity
- long-term interpretive stability

Naming shall not be treated as the sole basis of identity.

## Artifact Numbering

Artifact numbering means the governed ordinal or structural placement convention by which artifacts are positioned within constitutional families, milestone sequences, or governed series.

Numbering exists to preserve structural legibility and series continuity.

Numbering shall support:
- family continuity
- milestone ordering
- discoverability
- governance reference stability

Numbering shall not override constitutional subject distinction.

## Artifact Families

Artifact families are governed constitutional groupings whose members share a common constitutional class, review pattern, or publication role.

Family membership exists to preserve order and intelligibility across large constitutional programs.

Artifact family membership shall not erase individual subject identity.

## Multi-Part Artifacts

A multi-part artifact is a single constitutional subject intentionally expressed across multiple governed parts.

Multi-part doctrine exists where one constitutional subject cannot remain intelligible within a single bounded artifact without loss of structure.

A multi-part artifact shall preserve:
- one governing subject
- explicit internal part order
- interpretive continuity across parts
- publication discoverability as both whole and part

A multi-part artifact shall not be confused with a collection of independent artifacts.

## Composite Artifacts

A composite artifact is a governed publication unit composed of multiple constitutionally distinct artifacts whose coordinated presence is required for complete comprehension, review, or release.

Composite doctrine exists to preserve milestone-level and package-level intelligibility.

Composite artifacts shall preserve the distinction between:
- the composite publication unit
- the individual constituent artifact identities

## Successor Artifacts

A successor artifact is a governed constitutional artifact that inherits the active constitutional role previously held by another artifact.

Successor doctrine exists to preserve continuity across transformation without collapsing history.

A successor shall:
- be explicit
- preserve predecessor intelligibility
- declare assumption of constitutional role
- support audit continuity

No successor relationship shall be inferred implicitly.

## Superseded Artifacts

A superseded artifact is a formerly active constitutional artifact whose governing role has been replaced by a successor or later authoritative artifact.

Supersession shall preserve:
- historical discoverability
- review history intelligibility
- publication history intelligibility
- lineage integrity

Supersession shall not erase constitutional identity.

## Aliases

An alias is a governed secondary name or reference by which a constitutional artifact may be known without changing its constitutional identity.

Alias doctrine exists to preserve navigability, historical compatibility, milestone continuity, and human discoverability.

Aliases shall never create identity multiplication.

Aliases shall remain subordinate to the primary constitutional subject.

## Identity Continuity

Identity continuity means the governed preservation of constitutional sameness across legitimate revision, review, publication, freezing, retirement, and supersession events.

Identity continuity shall distinguish clearly among:
- continued identity
- revised standing
- successor standing
- retired standing
- superseded standing

Identity continuity shall prevent the following constitutional defects:
- silent identity drift
- untraceable replacement
- ambiguous succession
- accidental artifact multiplication

## Constitutional Invariants

The constitutional identity model shall preserve the following invariants:
- every constitutional artifact is a distinct governed subject
- identity is constitutive, not incidental
- identity is immutable by constitutional rule
- identity survives publication change, review change, and repository relocation
- naming and numbering support identity but do not exhaust it
- successors and supersessions must be explicit
- aliases cannot create new governed subjects
- multi-part and composite forms must preserve subject intelligibility
- identity continuity must remain auditable

## Success Criteria

This specification is successful only if:
- every future constitutional artifact can be recognized as a distinct governed subject
- future registry systems can implement identity without redefining it
- future publication systems can publish artifacts without changing identity meaning
- future validators can test identity integrity without inventing identity doctrine
- future automation can reason about successors, aliases, and lineage without constitutional ambiguity
- identity remains stable across unlimited future constitutional growth
